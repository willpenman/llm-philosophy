# Forms of Mathematical Proof

## Core Logical Techniques
Direct proof | Assume hypotheses, apply definitions and logical steps to reach conclusion
Proof by contradiction (reductio ad absurdum) | Assume negation of conclusion, derive a logical contradiction
Proof by contrapositive | Prove ¬Q → ¬P instead of P → Q
Proof by cases (case analysis) | Partition into exhaustive cases, prove each separately
Proof by exhaustion (brute force) | Check every possible instance, often finitely many
Constructive proof (proof by exhibition) | Explicitly construct or display the object claimed to exist
Non-constructive existence proof | Prove something must exist without producing it
Proof by counterexample | Disprove a universal claim by exhibiting a specific failure
Uniqueness proof | Assume two objects satisfy the property, show they must be equal
Existence-and-uniqueness proof | Prove an object exists, then prove it is the only one
If-and-only-if proof (biconditional proof) | Prove forward direction and reverse direction separately
Proof by equivalence chain (iff chain) | Show P₁ ⇔ P₂ ⇔ … ⇔ Pₙ via successive equivalences
Proof by elimination | Rule out all possibilities except the desired conclusion
Vacuous proof | Show the hypothesis of P → Q is always false, making the implication trivially true
Trivial proof | Show the conclusion Q is always true regardless of P
Proof by WLOG (without loss of generality) | Reduce to a representative case via symmetry or relabeling

## Induction Family
Proof by weak (ordinary) mathematical induction | Prove P(base), then P(k) → P(k+1) for all k
Proof by strong (complete) induction | Prove P(base), then [P(base) ∧ … ∧ P(k)] → P(k+1)
Proof by mutual (simultaneous) induction | Induct on two or more statements in tandem, each helping prove the other
Proof by structural induction | Prove for base elements of a recursive structure, then for each constructor
Proof by transfinite induction | Prove base, successor, and limit-ordinal cases over all ordinals
Proof by well-founded induction | Induct over any well-founded relation: every nonempty subset has a minimal element
Proof by Noetherian induction | Induct using ascending/descending chain conditions on a poset
Proof by infinite descent (Fermat) | Assume a minimal positive solution exists, construct a strictly smaller one, contradiction
Proof by the well-ordering principle | Select the least element of a nonempty set of natural numbers, derive contradiction or property
Proof by induction on complexity/length/rank | Induct on a measure of structural complexity (formula length, derivation depth, rank, etc.)
Proof by course-of-values induction | Equivalent to strong induction; P(n) depends on all P(m) with m < n

## Formal Proof Systems and Logical Frameworks
Natural deduction proof | Apply introduction and elimination rules for each connective/quantifier
Sequent calculus proof (Gentzen-style) | Derive sequents Γ ⊢ Δ using structural and logical rules with cut
Hilbert-style axiomatic proof | Chain axiom instances with modus ponens (and generalization)
Proof by resolution | Convert to CNF, repeatedly resolve complementary literals until empty clause or saturation
Semantic tableau (truth tree) proof | Systematically decompose formulas on branches, close branches with contradictions
Proof by truth table | Evaluate all rows of a truth table to verify tautology/validity
Equational proof (equational reasoning) | Apply substitution and equational axioms to transform one expression into another
Proof by term rewriting | Apply oriented rewrite rules until reaching normal form, compare results
Proof by cut elimination | Remove cuts from a sequent proof, yielding a direct subformula-property proof
Proof by normalization (proof-theoretic) | Transform a natural deduction proof into normal form, eliminating detours
Proof by the Curry-Howard correspondence | Construct a typed lambda term inhabiting the proposition-as-type
Proof by realizability | Construct a computable realizer witnessing each quantifier in the statement

## Combinatorial and Discrete Methods
Combinatorial proof (counting two ways) | Show both sides of an identity count the same set of combinatorial objects
Double counting proof | Count one set by two methods, equate the results
Bijective proof | Construct an explicit bijection between two finite sets to show equal cardinality
Proof by the pigeonhole principle | Place n+1 objects into n boxes, conclude some box has ≥ 2
Proof by inclusion-exclusion | Compute a union's size by alternately adding and subtracting intersection sizes
Proof by Möbius inversion | Invert a sum over a poset using the Möbius function
Proof by generating functions | Encode a sequence as a formal power series, manipulate algebraically
Proof by the transfer matrix method | Encode walks/tilings as matrix powers, extract coefficients
Proof by the symbolic/analytic combinatorics method | Use singularity analysis of generating functions to extract asymptotics
Proof by recurrence relation | Establish and solve a recurrence that characterizes the quantity
Proof by the extremal principle | Consider the element maximizing/minimizing some quantity, derive contradiction or structure
Proof by greedy algorithm argument | Show a greedy strategy achieves optimum by exchange or stays-ahead argument
Proof by exchange argument | Show any optimal solution can be transformed to the greedy solution without loss
Proof by the Ramsey theory argument | Show sufficiently large structures must contain monochromatic substructures
Proof by regularity lemma (Szemerédi) | Partition a graph into pseudorandom-like parts, analyze the reduced graph
Proof by the container method | Show the family of independent sets is captured by a small number of containers
Proof by flag algebras | Use semidefinite programming on flag algebra inequalities
Proof by the discharging method | Assign charges, redistribute via rules, derive contradiction on total charge
Proof by coloring / labeling argument | Assign colors to elements, use constraints to derive impossibility or structure
Proof by parity argument | Track even/odd parity of some quantity to derive contradiction or necessary conditions
Proof by the handshaking lemma / degree-sum argument | Use the fact that the sum of degrees equals twice the number of edges
Proof by tiling/covering argument | Show a region can or cannot be tiled by certain pieces via invariants
Proof by a flow/network argument | Model as network flow, invoke max-flow min-cut or related theorems
Proof by matroid theory | Use matroid axioms, duality, or intersection to prove combinatorial results
Proof by topological combinatorics | Apply topological tools (Borsuk-Ulam, Lusternik-Schnirelmann) to combinatorial problems

## Probabilistic and Randomized Methods
Proof by the probabilistic method (Erdős) | Show that a random object has the desired property with positive probability
Proof by the first moment method (expectation) | Show E[X] > 0 (or < n) so some outcome achieves the desired bound
Proof by the second moment method (Paley-Zygmund) | Use variance bounds to show X > 0 with positive probability
Proof by the Lovász Local Lemma | Show bad events are sparse and weakly dependent, so all can be avoided simultaneously
Proof by the entropy method / compression | Use information-theoretic entropy to bound combinatorial quantities
Proof by the alteration (deletion) method | Randomly construct, then fix violations by deleting a few elements
Proof by derandomization (conditional expectations) | Convert probabilistic argument into deterministic one by fixing bits greedily
Proof by random restriction | Randomly restrict variables and analyze the simplified function
Proof by Kolmogorov complexity | Bound combinatorial quantities using incompressibility of random strings

## Algebraic Proof Techniques
Proof by direct algebraic manipulation / computation | Transform expressions using algebraic identities until reaching the result
Proof by the polynomial method | Encode the problem with polynomials, use degree or vanishing arguments
Proof by the dimension argument (linear algebra method) | Compare dimensions of vector spaces, apply rank-nullity or linear dependence
Proof by eigenvalue/spectral methods (algebraic) | Analyze eigenvalues of an associated matrix to bound combinatorial quantities
Proof by the trace method | Compute or bound the trace of a matrix to infer properties of eigenvalues
Proof by determinant argument | Evaluate or estimate a determinant to show (non)singularity or count
Proof by Gröbner basis computation | Compute a Gröbner basis to decide ideal membership or solve polynomial systems
Proof by resultants / elimination theory | Eliminate variables from polynomial systems via resultants
Proof by homomorphism / isomorphism theorems | Map between structures via a homomorphism, apply isomorphism theorems
Proof by quotient construction | Construct a quotient by an equivalence relation or ideal, derive properties
Proof by universal property / categorical adjunction | Characterize an object by its universal mapping property, deduce consequences
Proof by diagram chasing | Follow elements through a commutative diagram of exact sequences
Proof by exact sequence argument | Use short/long exact sequences to relate algebraic invariants
Proof by spectral sequence | Iteratively compute pages of a spectral sequence converging to the desired cohomology
Proof by derived functors (Ext, Tor, etc.) | Compute derived functors to extract algebraic or topological information
Proof by Galois theory | Use the Galois correspondence between subgroups and intermediate fields
Proof by representation theory | Analyze group actions via linear representations and their decompositions
Proof by character theory | Use character tables and orthogonality relations to prove results about groups
Proof by localization | Localize a ring/module at a prime, prove the local statement, reassemble
Proof by completion (algebraic) | Complete a local ring or metric space, prove the result in the completed setting
Proof by reduction mod p / finite field methods | Reduce an integer or algebraic problem modulo a prime, analyze over 𝔽ₚ
Proof by the Nullstellensatz | Relate polynomial vanishing to ideal membership via Hilbert's Nullstellensatz
Proof by descent (algebraic/Galois) | Use faithfully flat or Galois descent to transfer structure across base change

## Analytic Proof Techniques
Proof by epsilon-delta argument | Construct an explicit δ (or N) for each ε to establish limits, continuity, or convergence
Proof by the squeeze theorem / comparison | Bound a quantity between two quantities sharing the same limit
Proof by telescoping | Write a sum/product as a telescoping series where most terms cancel
Proof by Abel summation / summation by parts | Discrete analogue of integration by parts to estimate partial sums
Proof by partial fractions | Decompose a rational function, analyze each term separately
Proof by integral representation | Represent a quantity as an integral, then estimate or evaluate the integral
Proof by contour integration / residues | Integrate around a closed contour in ℂ, apply Cauchy's residue theorem
Proof by analytic continuation | Extend an analytic function beyond its original domain, use the extension
Proof by saddle-point / steepest descent method | Deform a contour to pass through a saddle point, extract leading asymptotics
Proof by stationary phase | Approximate oscillatory integrals by contribution near stationary points
Proof by Fourier analysis | Decompose into frequencies, use Parseval's theorem or decay of coefficients
Proof by Laplace / integral transform | Apply an integral transform, solve in transform domain, invert
Proof by the maximum/minimum principle | Use the fact that harmonic/subharmonic functions attain extrema on the boundary
Proof by comparison principle / barrier functions | Construct sub- or supersolutions that bound the true solution
Proof by energy method (PDE/analysis) | Define an energy functional, show it is monotone or bounded
Proof by variational method / calculus of variations | Minimize a functional to find solutions, prove existence of minimizers
Proof by monotone convergence theorem | Show an increasing sequence bounded above converges, take the limit
Proof by dominated convergence theorem | Bound integrands by an integrable function, pass limits inside the integral
Proof by uniform convergence argument | Establish uniform convergence to interchange limits with integrals/sums/derivatives
Proof by Arzelà-Ascoli compactness | Extract a convergent subsequence from equicontinuous, uniformly bounded functions
Proof by weak/weak-* compactness | Extract a weakly convergent subsequence in a reflexive Banach space
Proof by density / approximation | Prove for a dense subclass (smooth, simple, polynomial), extend by density
Proof by mollification / regularization | Convolve with a smooth kernel, prove for smooth approximations, take limits
Proof by interpolation (Riesz-Thorin, Marcinkiewicz) | Prove endpoint estimates, obtain intermediate results by interpolation
Proof by bootstrapping / regularity iteration | Start with weak regularity, iteratively improve until reaching desired smoothness
Proof by scaling / dimensional analysis | Exploit scale invariance to reduce parameters or normalize
Proof by blowup analysis | Rescale near a singularity, pass to a limit, derive contradiction from the blowup profile
Proof by the continuity method | Deform from a solvable problem to the target, show the set of solvable parameters is open and closed
Proof by the method of continuity / connectedness | Show a set is nonempty, open, and closed in a connected space, so it is everything
Proof by the implicit/inverse function theorem | Apply the IFT to deduce local existence and regularity of solutions
Proof by convexity (Jensen's inequality, etc.) | Exploit convexity/concavity of functions to derive inequalities
Proof by Lagrange multipliers / KKT conditions | Characterize extrema under constraints, derive necessary conditions
Proof by the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality | Apply |⟨u,v⟩| ≤ ‖u‖‖v‖ to bound sums, integrals, or inner products
Proof by AM-GM / power mean inequality | Apply arithmetic-geometric mean inequality or its generalizations
Proof by Schur convexity / majorization | Show a symmetric function is Schur-convex, optimize via majorization
Proof by the Baire category theorem | Show a set is residual (countable intersection of open dense sets) hence nonempty
Proof by measure-theoretic argument | Use σ-additivity, absolute continuity, or Radon-Nikodym to derive results
Proof by the Hahn-Banach theorem | Extend a functional or separate convex sets, derive dual characterizations

## Fixed-Point and Iterative Methods
Proof by the Banach fixed-point theorem (contraction mapping) | Show a map is a contraction, conclude a unique fixed point exists
Proof by Brouwer's fixed-point theorem | Continuous map on a compact convex set has a fixed point
Proof by Schauder's fixed-point theorem | Continuous map on a compact convex subset of a Banach space has a fixed point
Proof by the Knaster-Tarski fixed-point theorem | Monotone map on a complete lattice has a fixed point
Proof by Kakutani's fixed-point theorem | Upper hemicontinuous set-valued map on compact convex set has a fixed point
Proof by iteration / Picard iteration | Iterate a map, show convergence of successive approximations to a solution

## Probabilistic and Stochastic Methods
Proof by martingale argument | Show a process is a (super/sub)martingale, apply optional stopping or convergence
Proof by coupling | Construct two random processes on the same probability space, compare them directly
Proof by large deviations | Bound tail probabilities using exponential moment/rate function techniques
Proof by concentration inequalities (Chernoff, Azuma, Talagrand) | Bound deviation from mean using exponential or isoperimetric inequalities
Proof by Stein's method | Bound distance to a target distribution via a characterizing operator equation
Proof by moment method (probability) | Compute or bound moments, use moment convergence or moment comparison
Proof by characteristic function / Fourier method (probability) | Analyze distributions via their characteristic functions, use Lévy continuity
Proof by ergodic theory | Use ergodic theorems (Birkhoff, von Neumann) to derive long-run averages
Proof by stochastic calculus (Itô) | Apply Itô's formula to semimartingales, derive properties of SDEs

## Number-Theoretic Methods
Proof by modular arithmetic / congruence argument | Work modulo n to derive divisibility or impossibility results
Proof by Fermat's little theorem / Euler's theorem | Use aᵖ⁻¹ ≡ 1 mod p or related congruences
Proof by quadratic reciprocity | Apply quadratic reciprocity to determine solvability of quadratic congruences
Proof by p-adic analysis | Work in the p-adic numbers ℚₚ, use p-adic convergence or Hensel's lemma
Proof by the geometry of numbers (Minkowski) | Use lattice point theorems in convex bodies to show existence of solutions
Proof by the Hardy-Littlewood circle method | Decompose a generating function integral into major and minor arcs, estimate each
Proof by sieve methods (Brun, Selberg, large sieve) | Sieve out unwanted primes to bound counts of primes or almost-primes
Proof by L-functions / analytic number theory | Analyze L-functions (zeros, residues, functional equations) to extract arithmetic info
Proof by algebraic number theory (ideal theory, class groups) | Use unique factorization of ideals, class numbers, units to derive results
Proof by continued fraction expansion | Represent numbers as continued fractions, use convergent properties
Proof by Vieta jumping / root flipping | In a Diophantine equation, treat one variable as a root and jump to the conjugate root

## Geometric Methods
Proof by synthetic (Euclidean) geometry | Use axioms, congruence, similarity, and classical theorems directly
Proof by analytic/coordinate geometry | Assign coordinates, reduce geometric statements to algebraic equations
Proof by geometric transformation (symmetry, rotation, reflection, translation) | Apply a transformation to map one configuration to another
Proof by inversion (inversive geometry) | Invert through a circle/sphere to simplify tangency or angle conditions
Proof by projective geometry | Work in projective space, use cross-ratios, duality, perspectivities
Proof by affine methods | Use affine invariants and affine transformations
Proof by barycentric coordinates | Express points as weighted averages of triangle vertices, compute algebraically
Proof by trigonometric (trigonometric cevian/identity) methods | Apply trig identities, law of sines/cosines, to establish geometric facts
Proof by vector methods | Use vector addition, dot products, cross products to prove geometric statements
Proof by complex numbers in geometry | Represent plane points as complex numbers, use multiplication and conjugation
Proof by dissection / decomposition | Cut a figure into pieces and rearrange to show area/volume equality
Proof by geometric series / recursion | Use self-similarity of a geometric construction to set up a geometric series

## Topological Methods
Proof by compactness (topological) | Extract finite subcovers, or use sequential compactness to find convergent subsequences
Proof by connectedness / path-connectedness | Show a set is connected to prove it cannot be split, or use IVT
Proof by the fundamental group / covering spaces | Compute π₁, use lifting properties of covering spaces
Proof by homology theory | Compute homology groups, use exact sequences and functoriality
Proof by cohomology theory | Compute cohomology, use cup products or cohomological operations
Proof by homotopy theory | Use homotopy groups, fibrations, or homotopy equivalences
Proof by degree theory (topological degree) | Compute the degree of a map, use homotopy invariance to derive existence
Proof by index theory (Atiyah-Singer, Lefschetz, etc.) | Compute an index or fixed-point count via topological invariants
Proof by Euler characteristic / Gauss-Bonnet | Compute χ or apply Gauss-Bonnet to relate topology and curvature
Proof by Morse theory | Analyze critical points of a smooth function to determine topological structure
Proof by surgery theory | Cut and reglue manifolds, track how invariants change
Proof by cobordism | Show two manifolds are cobordant, use cobordism invariants
Proof by characteristic classes (Chern, Stiefel-Whitney, Pontryagin) | Compute characteristic classes to distinguish or obstruct
Proof by K-theory | Use K-groups of vector bundles or C*-algebras to derive invariants
Proof by sheaf theory / sheaf cohomology | Compute sheaf cohomology groups, use exact sequences of sheaves
Proof by the Borsuk-Ulam theorem | Show an antipodal map must have a coincidence point
Proof by the Lefschetz fixed-point theorem | If the Lefschetz number is nonzero, the map has a fixed point
Proof by obstruction theory | Identify topological obstructions (cohomology classes) to extending maps

## Set-Theoretic and Model-Theoretic Methods
Proof by forcing (Cohen forcing, iterated forcing) | Extend a model of ZFC with generic filters to establish consistency/independence
Proof by inner models (L, HOD, core models) | Work inside a canonical inner model to establish consistency results
Proof by large cardinal axioms | Assume existence of large cardinals, derive consequences via their reflection properties
Proof by determinacy (AD, PD) | Assume determinacy of certain games, derive regularity properties of sets
Proof by absoluteness (Shoenfield, Mansfield) | Show a statement is absolute between models, transfer results between them
Proof by the reflection principle (set-theoretic) | Reflect a property of V down to some Vα, use the reflected version
Proof by club and stationary set arguments | Use the closed unbounded filter and stationary sets to argue about uncountable combinatorics
Proof by Zorn's lemma / axiom of choice / well-ordering theorem | Invoke Zorn to find maximal elements, or well-order to make transfinite choices
Proof by cardinal arithmetic | Use properties of cardinal exponentiation, cofinality, GCH/SCH
Proof by the ultrafilter lemma | Use existence of ultrafilters (weaker than AC) to derive compactness-type results
Proof by ultraproducts / Łoś's theorem | Construct an ultraproduct, apply Łoś to transfer first-order properties
Proof by the back-and-forth method | Build an isomorphism incrementally, alternating between the two structures
Proof by Löwenheim-Skolem | Obtain models of different cardinalities, use to prove or disprove properties
Proof by compactness (model-theoretic) | If every finite subset of a theory is satisfiable, the whole theory is
Proof by quantifier elimination | Eliminate quantifiers to reduce to quantifier-free statements, then check directly
Proof by model completeness | Show every embedding between models is elementary, derive consequences
Proof by o-minimality | Work in an o-minimal structure where definable sets are finite unions of intervals
Proof by the transfer principle (nonstandard analysis) | Transfer first-order statements between standard and nonstandard models
Proof by omitting types | Apply the omitting types theorem to build models missing certain types
Proof by indiscernibles / Ramsey-model-theory | Use Ehrenfeucht-Mostowski models built from indiscernible sequences

## Category-Theoretic Methods
Proof by the Yoneda lemma | Reduce to checking natural transformations from representable functors
Proof by adjoint functor arguments | Use existence/properties of adjoint functors to derive results
Proof by limits/colimits and universal constructions | Construct objects via universal properties, prove results via their characterization
Proof by topos-theoretic internal logic | Interpret the statement in the internal language of a topos, prove it there

## Computability and Complexity Methods
Proof by diagonalization (Cantor / Turing) | Construct an object differing from the nth listed object at the nth position
Proof by reduction (computability) | Reduce a known undecidable problem to the target problem
Proof by the halting problem / Rice's theorem | Show solving the problem would solve the halting problem
Proof by NP-hardness / complexity reduction | Reduce a known NP-hard problem to the target via polynomial-time mapping
Proof by communication complexity | Model as a communication game, prove lower bounds on bits exchanged
Proof by information-theoretic lower bound | Use entropy or mutual information to prove impossibility or lower bounds
Proof by circuit complexity / gate elimination | Analyze Boolean circuits, derive lower bounds on size or depth
Proof by the adversary method | Show any algorithm can be forced into many queries by an adversary

## Computational and Machine-Assisted Methods
Computer-assisted proof (exhaustive case checking) | Use a computer to verify finitely many cases too numerous for humans (e.g., Four Color Theorem)
Proof by formal verification / proof assistant (Coq, Lean, Isabelle, HOL) | Encode every step in a formal system, verified correct by software
Proof by SAT solver | Encode the problem as a Boolean satisfiability instance, solve, extract proof/certificate
Proof by SMT solver | Encode in a satisfiability modulo theories framework, obtain a verified result
Proof by model checking | Exhaustively verify all states of a finite-state system against a specification
Proof by certified / verified computation | Perform a numerical computation with a machine-checkable correctness certificate
Proof by interval arithmetic | Bound all intermediate values in rigorous intervals, verify the conclusion holds

## General Proof Strategies and Meta-Methods
Proof by symmetry | Exploit a symmetry of the problem to reduce cases or equate quantities
Proof by duality | Pass to a dual formulation (e.g., planar graph duality, LP duality, Pontryagin duality), prove the dual statement
Proof by generalization (strengthening the hypothesis) | Prove a stronger result whose inductive structure or applicability is cleaner
Proof by specialization | Prove a specific case that implies or illuminates the general result
Proof by reduction to a known result | Transform the problem until a known theorem directly applies
Proof by analogy / transport of structure | Establish a formal correspondence and transfer a known proof across it
Proof by the probabilistic lens (reformulation) | Reinterpret a deterministic statement probabilistically to gain insight or tools
Proof by phantom / auxiliary construction | Introduce a new object (auxiliary line, helper function, extension field) that clarifies the argument
Proof without words (visual / diagrammatic proof) | Present a diagram or picture that makes the argument self-evident
Proof by wishful thinking / reverse engineering | Assume the answer has a certain form, determine parameters, then verify rigorously
